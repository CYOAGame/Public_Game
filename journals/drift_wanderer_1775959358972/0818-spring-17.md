# Drift the Wanderer

**Spring, Day 17, Year 818**

You reported the corruption. The captain of the guard has opened an investigation and wants a word with you.

Brenna summons you to the guardhouse. It is spare and clean, the kind of space that belongs to someone who means what they say. The captain closes the door and sits across from you. "Voss denies everything. Their record is clean on paper." A pause. "Tell me exactly what happened. Spare nothing." The ledger is open. The quill is in hand. This is a formal proceeding.

> Tell the complete truth. Every word, every detail.

Brenna writes without looking up, then sets the quill down. "This is enough to open a formal inquiry." They meet your eyes. "You understand this may make you a target. Voss has friends and the kind of friends who do small, difficult-to-prove things." A long silence. "I will have two guards on your door tonight. Whether you want them or not, they are there."

> Accept the protection gratefully

The inquiry is running. Voss has been moved to desk duties, which is a formal signal even if it is not yet punishment. You are aware of them at the edge of things: in the lane, in the market, watching without looking directly. It will take three days for the inquiry to run its formal course. Three days is a long time to be the person who filed the complaint.

> Stay steady. Go about your days normally. Refuse to be moved.

The inquiry finds. The outcome depends on the strength of the account and the evidence behind it: Voss is either suspended, reassigned, formally cautioned, or, with enough evidence, dismissed and referred for prosecution. The captain informs you of the outcome in writing, a short letter that says more in its formality than in its words. Whatever happened, the mechanism worked or it did not, and you know now how much the mechanism is worth in this town.

> Accept the outcome. Justice of some kind was attempted.

There is still daylight. The story is not yet finished.

> Tell the captain you know of others who may be willing to testify

Brenna straightens. "Other witnesses." They write something new in the ledger. "If you can bring them to me directly, or name them and I will send for them quietly, that changes what kind of inquiry this is. One account is a complaint. Three accounts is a pattern. Patterns are much harder to dismiss." They look at you. "Can you get them to speak?"

> Say yes. You know who to approach and how.

The formal inquiry convenes: Brenna, the alderman's clerk, and a panel of two. You are asked to give your account formally. Voss is present, on the other side of the table, with the particular stillness of someone who is very angry and being careful about it. The process is dry and methodical and more unsettling for being so.
