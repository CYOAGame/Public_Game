# Drift the Wanderer

**Winter, Day 18, Year 837**

You wake before dawn. The air is cool, the village is quiet, and pale light creeps through the shutters. You lie still for a moment, listening to the town breathe. Somewhere outside: a dog barking twice and then stopping. The well-rope groaning in the wind. A morning like any other, except for something you cannot name, a low-grade alertness behind your sternum.

> Sit by the window and watch the village wake

The village stirs slowly. A baker crosses the square with a crate of loaves. A cat sits on the granary steps, unimpressed. Then you see Lydia cutting a quick diagonal across the square, looking over their shoulder in a way that snags your attention. They glance up at your window. They see you. They stop and beckon urgently, then hold a finger to their lips.

> Hold up a hand, signal them to wait while you pull on your boots

Lydia speaks in a low, rapid rush. Last night, just past the second watch-bell, they saw three lights moving across the ridge above the Ashwood. Slow, deliberate, too evenly spaced to be animals. They came down toward the old shepherd track and then simply stopped. Went out one by one. "I went up this morning before you were awake," Lydia says. "There is something there. Marks in the ground. I did not go close." They are watching your face carefully.

> Ask who else they have told

Lydia hesitates. "No one. I came straight to you." Something in their voice says that was a considered decision, not a convenient one. "I did not want to cause a fuss if it is nothing. But I also did not want to go back up there alone." They pause. "And, I have seen you pay attention to things most people walk past."

> Accept that, go up to the ridge with them

The shepherd track is wet with dew. Lydia leads you to a flat section of ground just below the ridgeline. The marks are unmistakable once you see them: three shallow circles, each about a foot across, pressed into the soft earth in a perfect equilateral triangle. The grass inside each circle is yellowed. Dead, but not burned. In the centre of the triangle, a single smooth stone that was not there before, you can see the undisturbed moss around where it was placed. Lydia grips your arm. "Someone put that there on purpose."

> Search the surrounding area for more signs before leaving

Twenty minutes of careful looking turns up two more marks, further apart, less clear, as if the triangle you found is only one part of a larger pattern. You pace out the distances. If the geometry holds, the full shape would encompass most of the north slope of the ridge. Whatever was done here was not small. Lydia has gone pale and quiet. "We should go back down," they say. "Now."

> Agree, you have enough for now

By the time you are back in the square, the village is fully awake. Smoke from the bakehouse, the clang of the smithy starting up, children sent for water. Lydia walks beside you for a while without speaking. Before they peel off toward their own door, they say quietly: "Thank you. For coming with me. For not thinking I was mad." You watch them go, the morning pressing in around you with its ordinary noise, the ridge behind the roofline, out of sight.

> Go home and write down every detail while it is fresh

The notes sit on your table: three circles in an equilateral triangle, yellowed grass, a smooth stone with a mark. You know the person in town most likely to have seen something like this: the old record-keeper who keeps ledgers going back six generations and has opinions about most of them. You take the sketch there. They put on their spectacles. A long silence. "I have seen this configuration once before," they say. "In a margin note. Forty years ago. Where did you find this?"

> Tell them about the stone but not the lights, not yet

The record-keeper finds the margin note in a ledger from the year of the Long Frost. A traveler passed through, described as "a surveyor of old places," and asked about the ridge. The note says the traveler left a mark on a stone "as a waypoint." The handwriting is shaky but the implication is clear: someone has been using the ridge as a landmark for at least forty years. Someone who was passing through then is passing through again now.

> Ask the record-keeper if there is anything else about the traveler

The morning resolves into afternoon and then evening. The village does what villages do: bread is made, animals are fed, small arguments are had and mostly forgotten. Lydia waves at you across the square with an expression that says they have been thinking about the ridge too. The stone or its sketch or its absence sits with you. Whatever began this morning has not ended. It has only paused.

> Go back to the ridge at dusk to see if the lights return

The day draws to a close.
