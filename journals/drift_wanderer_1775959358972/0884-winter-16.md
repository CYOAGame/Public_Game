# Drift the Wanderer

**Winter, Day 16, Year 884**

A stranger slips through the town gate just before dusk, dusty, limping, clutching a worn leather satchel to their chest. Patch scans the square with wide, frightened eyes and fixes on you. Not on the nearest face. On yours specifically. "Please," they rasp. "I need somewhere safe. Just for tonight." Behind them, Aldric is watching from the gatehouse. The gate will close in minutes.

> Bring them to Aldric for proper protection

Aldric listens, nods, and takes Patch firmly by the arm. "You did right bringing them in," the guard says. "We will look after this." Patch shoots you a look, not quite betrayal, not quite anger, something cooler and more considered. As they are led away with the satchel still tucked under their arm, they say quietly, just to you: "If they open the satchel, tell them not to open it alone."

> Catch up to Aldric and pass on the traveler's warning

Aldric pauses when you relay the warning. Their expression shifts, just slightly, from bureaucratic patience to something more alert. "Not open it alone," they repeat. "Interesting thing to say." They study you. "How well did you know this person before tonight?" The question is sharp and you did not expect it.

> Tell them honestly you had never met Patch before tonight

Morning. You check on Patch, wherever they ended up, the cooperage or the guardhouse waiting room if the guard proved accommodating. They look rested in the way of someone who was desperate for sleep and finally got it. The satchel is in their lap. They have been awake for a while, thinking. When they see you, something shifts in their posture, the decision to trust fully made, or renewed. They begin to speak.

> Sit down and listen to everything they want to tell you

Patch speaks carefully, choosing each word. The satchel contains documents that could ruin a name in this town. They copied them from a ledger they had legitimate access to. "Account records," they say. "A diversion of funds. Three towns, including Ironhaven. The name on the account has standing here." They stop. "I am not a thief. I am a clerk who made a copy and ran when the wrong person saw me do it. The men sent after me are a day behind, at most." The words land quietly and settle heavily.

> Ask whose name is on the account

Patch tells you the name. You recognize it. Of course you do. It is the kind of name that appears on dedication plaques and civic records, attached to generous donations and public goodwill. The name of someone who has been building a reputation for years, carefully, on a foundation that is apparently stolen. You sit with that for a moment.

> Say you need to see the documents themselves before deciding anything

Patch hesitates, then opens the satchel. Inside, wrapped in oilcloth: a sheaf of ledger copies and a folded letter bearing a crest you know. The numbers are meticulous. The diversions are systematic, running back four years. The letter is more damning still, written in a familiar hand, addressed to someone outside town, referring to a fund as "our account" with a familiarity that assumes shared guilt. Your hands are steady as you reseal the oilcloth. Your mind is not.

> Close the satchel and tell them you are in

"I need the documents to reach someone who cannot be bought," Patch says. "The miller's widow on the east road. She was a magistrate's clerk twenty years ago. She knows what to do with evidence." They press a small sealed letter into your hand. "That is for her. The satchel stays with me until I know the way is clear. But I need someone who knows this town." They look at you steadily. "Can you get word to her?"

> Agree to carry word to the miller's widow

By mid-morning, two riders are in the square. Well-dressed, unhurried, asking quiet questions of stall-holders. Not guards. Not merchants. Their horses are good and their hands say they know how to use what is at their belts. Someone has already pointed in your direction. You can see it in the way one of them scans the square and pauses when their eyes find you. Patch is not in sight. You have perhaps a minute before one of them crosses the square.
