# Drift the Wanderer

**Winter, Day 2, Year 892**

Something strange has been following you since the shrine. The wise woman says she knows what it means.

It has been a week since the shrine. Strange things, small ones: your tools stay sharp past reason. Bread you baked lasts twice as long. A dog follows you for two days and then disappears. At night, you dream of roots and stone and something watching from below the earth. Hilda stops you in the lane. "Something has marked you," she says without greeting. "I can see it. The question is whether it means you well."

> Ask Hilda to help you understand what happened

Hilda sits with you for an hour, asking questions. Did you take something from the shrine? Did you leave an offering? Were you alone? Her expression grows thoughtful rather than alarmed. "It is old power," she says finally. "Not evil. But it has a price. Things that come easily start to cost you elsewhere. Be careful what you use it for." She pauses. "And it is not permanent. If you do not feed it attention, it will fade. Three months, maybe four."

> Heed the warning, use whatever gift this is sparingly

You understand the marking now, at least what Hilda understands of it. The benefits are real. The costs are subtle. And the presence watching through you has a purpose that may or may not align with your own. You have several directions you could go from here.

> Tell Hilda you want to talk to the wandering priest about this

The priest listens to everything you tell them about the marking with the focus of someone hearing a confirmation of something they already suspected. "The binding is looking for a guardian," they say. "I have seen this pattern before. The marking is not a curse. It is an audition." They fold their hands. "Whether you accept the role is yours to decide. But you should know: if you do not, it will find someone else. It will keep trying until the town falls or someone tends it."

> Accept the keeper role after hearing the priest's account

The first weeks of tending feel strange. You visit the shrine when you can, attend to it the way you would a garden: clearing, checking, leaving things as you found them but with attention added. Hilda tells you that is probably enough. "The previous keeper did not do anything dramatic," she says. "They just paid attention. The binding does the work. It needs someone to notice when the work is slipping." You are learning what slipping looks like. It is subtle. You will keep learning.

> Ask Hilda to teach you more about what to look for when tending

Hilda agrees to teach, but her teaching is not instruction. It is observation. She comes to the shrine with you once, says almost nothing, and on the walk back tells you three things to watch for: a change in the quality of silence near the door, a smell of iron that comes and goes, and a particular kind of dream that is not a dream but a communication. "You will know the difference," she says, "because you will remember it perfectly in the morning. Most dreams fade. These do not."

> Trust her guidance. You will learn to read the signs she described.

There is still daylight. The story is not yet finished.
