# Patch the Beggar

**Spring, Day 1, Year 845**

Morning light falls flat across the square. You are barely awake when the shouting begins Voss's voice cutting through the ordinary noise like a blade. A crowd forms fast, the way crowds do when there is something sharp to gather around. At the center: Voss, red-faced and righteous, dragging Lydia toward the steps where Livia sits in a chair carried out for the purpose. The charge is already spreading mouth to mouth: theft from the town stores. Stealing grain during the shortage.

> Push through the crowd to get closer you know Lydia

Lydia is protesting voice cracked, hands out, the posture of someone trying not to seem desperate and failing. Voss holds up a sack of grain and turns it in the air so the crowd can see the store mark stamped on the burlap. "Found in their home. In their cellar." The crowd murmurs and shifts. Some faces you recognize. Most of them are already deciding. Livia sits very still, watching everything, giving nothing away.

> Step forward "I know Lydia. This is wrong."

You step forward and the crowd parts slightly, the way crowds do when someone commits to a position. "I know Lydia. They have lived in this town for years. They fed people during the last shortage, not stole from them." Voss turns to you with a look you will remember patient, almost amused. "Then explain the grain," they say. "Explain the store mark. Explain why it was in their cellar." The crowd waits. Livia is watching you now.

> I cannot explain the grain. But I know this person. Something is wrong here.

You hold your ground and hold your tongue. Livia calls for anyone with knowledge of the matter to come forward before the trial proper which will be held at noon. The crowd begins to break apart, people taking their interpretations with them. As the square empties you see Hilda still standing near the well, not moving, arms wrapped around themselves despite the warmth of the morning. They look at you. It lasts a second longer than a stranger's glance would.

> Walk toward Hilda that look meant something

Hilda does not run, but they hold themselves very still as you close the distance, the way an animal stays still hoping not to be seen. When you are close enough to speak quietly, they speak first barely above a breath. "I saw something that night. What they said about Lydia." A pause. They look at the ground, then back at you. "It wasn't them. But I cannot say it here. Not in the open. Not where people would hear who said it."

> Then say it to me. Just me. Right now.

Hilda shakes their head and takes a small step back. "My family works the guard rotation schedule. Three of us. If I open my mouth and Voss hears about it and they will that ends." They say it flatly, the way someone describes weather. Not a threat, just a fact they have been carrying for hours. "I am not a coward. I just need it to matter if I speak. I need to know someone will do something with what I say."

> You cannot promise that. You say nothing.

The morning moves toward noon. The trial will happen whether you are ready or not. You have time for two or three more threads before Livia convenes the square. Lydia is being held at the back of the guard post. The town stores are unlocked and accessible. Hilda can be found at the church if you look. Voss is visible near the square, accepting the handshakes of people who have already decided. The grain sack evidence is sitting on the magistrate's table unattended.

> Find Hilda behind the church

Behind the church is a narrow space between the building and the cemetery wall, out of sightline of the square. Hilda is already there when you arrive, which tells you they have been thinking about this meeting since the well. They speak without preamble. "I saw Voss carrying a sack from the direction of the stores to the lane behind Lydia's house. Before dawn. I could not sleep. I was at the window." They say it steadily. "It was the same sack. I know the mark on it. I have bought from those stores."

> Thank them and ask them to stay quiet for now you need to think

> Examine the grain sack on the magistrate's table

The grain sack sits on the edge of the magistrate's table, alone in the way that pieces of evidence are always alone suddenly significant, loaded with meaning they did not have an hour ago. You pick it up. Turn it. The store mark is clear. Then something else: near the bottom of the burlap, a smudge pressed into the weave. It took a moment to read. A seal impression partial, blurred, but the shape is there. A ring pressed into wet grain dust. The shape of it matches the kind of ring a guard officer wears on their right hand.

> Set the sack down carefully and take nothing. File what you saw.
