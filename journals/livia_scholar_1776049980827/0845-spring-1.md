# Livia the Scholar

**Spring, Day 1, Year 845**

Rosie arrives at your study with a wooden case that has been locked in a way that suggests both value and anxiety. Inside is a manuscript, not a codex but a scroll, old enough that the vellum has taken on a color between amber and bone. The script is not a language you know immediately, but it is not entirely unfamiliar: elements of it are related to a southern trade script you studied fifteen years ago, and there are logograms in the margin that you recognize from a completely different tradition. Rosie watches your face. "Can you read it?" They are hoping for a simple yes.

> Ask to keep it overnight to compare against your references before committing

The first three sections are geographical records, detailed and specific, describing a route through terrain you recognize as the borderlands to the north. The fourth section changes register entirely, and you stop. What you are reading is an account of an event, described in careful, clinical language, that cannot be reconciled with anything you were taught. Not because it is fantastical, but because it is precise, the way eyewitness accounts are precise, with distances and times and the names of people who were present. The names are names you recognize from other sources. This text predates those sources by three centuries.

> Continue translating. You need the full text before you can understand it.

The full translation takes two days. What it says changes your understanding of several things you thought you understood. It also explains the strange geological feature in the northern valley that three different expeditions have noted without explaining, and it names a location specifically enough that, with the right instruments and the right preparation, someone could find it. Someone with this translation could find it. You sit back and look at Rosie across the study and realize, for the first time, that you do not know who they are or why they have this text.

> Give a translation that omits the specific location details

Knowledge that changes everything is a particular kind of weight to carry. You have encountered it before, the discovery that revises context rather than just adding to it, and each time it raises the same question: who has the right to know this, and who has the right to decide who knows it. The scholars' guild has a protocol for such things. The protocol exists because people who do not follow it have, historically, created problems that outlasted them by generations.
